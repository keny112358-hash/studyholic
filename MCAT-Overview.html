What is the MCAT?

The MCAT, or Medical College Admission Test, is a standardized exam created by the AAMC (Association of American Medical Colleges) to evaluate knowledge and skills for medical school admission.

Exam Structure

The MCAT consists of four sections: Biological Sciences, Chemical and Physical Foundations, Psychological and Social Foundations, and Critical Analysis and Reasoning Skills, each with multiple-choice questions.

Test Duration and Timing

The full MCAT lasts approximately 7 hours and 30 minutes, including optional breaks. Each section is timed as follows:

  • Biological and Biochemical Foundations: 95 minutes
  • Chemical and Physical Foundations: 95 minutes
  • Psychological, Social, and Biological Foundations: 95 minutes
  • Critical Analysis and Reasoning Skills: 90 minutes

There are short breaks between sections and a longer mid-exam break. Practicing under timed conditions is essential to manage energy and pacing on test day.

Purpose of the Exam

The MCAT evaluates readiness for medical school, assessing critical thinking, problem-solving, and knowledge in science and social sciences for admissions decisions.
